Theory Guide

Roman Numerals — I, ii, IV, V…

Each number represents a scale degree. UPPERCASE = major chord, lowercase = minor, ° = diminished. The same progression (e.g. I–V–vi–IV) works in any key.

ITonic — home base, most stable iiSupertonic — mild tension, leads to V iiiMediant — emotional colour, used sparingly IVSubdominant — warm, stable, contra-home VDominant — strong pull back to I (tension) viSubmediant — emotional relative minor of I vii°Leading tone — maximum tension, wants to resolve

How the Circle Visualises This

When you select a key, the orange arc is your I chord (home). The green arc clockwise is your V chord (dominant — creates tension). The blue arc counter-clockwise is your IV chord (subdominant — warm and stable).

Moving counter-clockwise (IV→I→V) resolves tension. Moving clockwise (I→V) builds tension. Jazz uses ii→V→I — three steps counter-clockwise. The circle makes this geography visible.

Why Major Chords Are Visually Brighter

The outer (major) ring uses bolder colours because major keys are the traditional "tonal centres" of Western harmony — they're the primary harmonic home. Minor chords (inner ring) are secondary — emotionally darker, used for contrast or depth. The visual weight mirrors the harmonic weight.

Tip: Click the inner ring to switch to a minor key perspective. The chord grid recalculates with minor Roman numerals (i, ii°, III, iv, v, VI, VII) and the natural minor scale.
